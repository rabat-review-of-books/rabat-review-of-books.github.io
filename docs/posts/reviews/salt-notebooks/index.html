The Divided Ledger

Salima Oumari’s debut wrestles with inheritance and estrangement.

Reviews
Fiction
Oumari’s The Salt Notebooks charts a woman’s return to her fractured family across two countries.
Author

Youssef Benani

Published

March 15, 2026

The Salt Notebooks by Salima Oumari · Éditions du Détroit · 412 pp · €26

Salima Oumari’s debut novel arrives trailing the kind of acclaim that often precedes disappointment. Longlisted for the Prix Goncourt, interviewed in Libération, hailed as a vital voice on the postcolonial diaspora—the machinery of French literary celebrity had already begun processing The Salt Notebooks before most readers outside Paris had opened it. So it is perhaps to Oumari’s credit that the novel proves more interesting than such machinery might suggest, though rarely in the ways its admirers intend.

The book follows Leila, a forty-three-year-old translator living in Lyon, who receives word that her father—whom she has not seen in seventeen years—is dying in Casablanca. The premise is familiar enough: exile, return, reckoning with origins. What distinguishes Oumari’s approach is her refusal to sentimentalize either gesture. Leila does not rush home. She prevaricates, translates a minor Moroccan poet’s work, attends dinner parties where she is asked about her “authentic” childhood. Only when her father lapses into silence does she make the journey.

The novel’s architecture, however, is its most intriguing formal choice. Interspersed with the contemporary narrative are fragments—letters, grocery lists, medical notes, overheard conversations—attributed to her father’s notebooks spanning forty years. These are not straightforwardly “his voice.” Instead, Oumari renders them as textual residue, the detritus of a life recorded without coherence or emotional clarity. A notebook entry from 1987 reads, paraphrased, something like: The plumber came. Leila asked for money for school. Too expensive. We ate bread with oil. The flatness is deliberate. It refuses the pathos that a more sentimental novelist would extract from such scenes.

This method both succeeds and falters. The accumulated weight of these fragments does eventually create a portrait—of a man constrained by circumstance, diminished by migration, bewildered by his own daughter—that is more unsettling than any summary could be. We see not the father Leila imagines but the mundane, inarticulate reality of his daily existence. Yet Oumari sometimes overplays this technique. By the novel’s final third, the fragments become repetitive, almost mechanical. We need not be told twelve times that the family was poor; we understood after three.

The contemporary storyline, by contrast, risks smoothness. Leila’s reunion with her ailing father occurs in a hospital room where dialogue has largely been replaced by silence and the ambient sounds of medical equipment. It is a quiet, almost minimalist sequence, and there is dignity in Oumari’s refusal to engineer reconciliation or epiphany. But this restraint can feel like evasion. We learn little of what actually transpired between them, why the rupture was so absolute, what words (if any) are exchanged at the end. Oumari seems to believe that suggesting absence is more powerful than exploring it—a choice that occasionally leaves the reader stranded in a void of her own creation.

What emerges most forcefully is not a family drama but a meditation on translation itself. Leila is a translator by profession, and the novel is haunted by the question of what can and cannot be conveyed between languages, generations, and the living and the dead. When she attempts to translate her father’s notebooks (a task we never see completed), she finds herself inventing, interpreting, filling gaps. The parallel to her own attempts to “translate” her childhood, her father, herself becomes explicit. Yet the novel hints that perfect translation is impossible—that what survives the crossing always arrives corrupted, changed, remade.

This is sophisticated work, though not always satisfying. Oumari writes with clarity and occasional beauty, and her ear for the rhythms of French and Arabic inflected speech is acute. There are moments—a description of the Casablanca medina, a conversation between Leila and a fellow translator—where the prose achieves a kind of luminous precision. But too often the novel settles for gestures toward depth rather than depth itself. We are told repeatedly that Leila is conflicted, divided, estranged; we are shown this less often than we might like.

Still, The Salt Notebooks announces an author uninterested in easy answers, content to sit with ambiguity and silence. In an era of literary maximalism, that may itself be a kind of courage. Whether it amounts to wisdom remains a question for readers to settle themselves.

Youssef Benani teaches comparative literature at the University of Fez and writes regularly on contemporary Francophone fiction. His essay collection, “Reading at the Margins,” will be published next year.
