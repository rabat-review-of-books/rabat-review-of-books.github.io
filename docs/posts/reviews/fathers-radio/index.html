Static and Signal

A Cairo childhood emerges through the voices of radio.

Reviews
Memoir
Laila Mahmoud’s My Father’s Radio uses a single object to illuminate urban memory and family rupture.
Author

Mehdi Khribch

Published

April 3, 2026

My Father’s Radio by Laila Mahmoud · Dar Merit · 288 pp · 150 EGP

The radio in question is not particularly distinguished. A Philips set from the 1960s, purchased by Laila Mahmoud’s father, Omar, when he arrived in Cairo from the Delta as a young man seeking work in the new industrial sectors. It was a marker of aspiration, of a modernized Egypt. The radio accompanied the family through the Suez Crisis, Nasser’s rise, the early years of Sadat, and eventually into a period when it became, as Mahmoud writes, “less an object of longing and more a relic in a house already becoming haunted by memory.”

This is the occasion for Mahmoud’s memoir, My Father’s Radio—though calling it a memoir in the conventional sense is misleading. It is not a chronological narrative of a life. Instead, it is a series of fragments, radio memories, overheard conversations, and interpolations on Cairo itself, all arranged around this single object. The radio is rarely the explicit subject. Instead, it functions as what we might call a memory-machine, a device through which other experiences acquire their particular shape and resonance.

Mahmoud grew up in Heliopolis, the suburban quarter built in the early twentieth century, and she has a sharp eye for the textures of that world: the apartment blocks slowly decaying, the streets at dusk, the particular rhythms of neighborhood life that have now mostly vanished. The radio occupies the sitting room (what she calls the “best room,” kept at a certain distance from the life of the house), and it becomes the place where her father vanishes into the voices of news broadcasters, Qur’an reciters, and the radio theater that was still a significant form of entertainment in Cairo in the 1970s.

What makes the book compelling is not any singular revelation but the gradual accumulation of a presence through absence. Omar emerges from these pages not as a fully realized character—Mahmoud is not interested in that kind of psychological portraiture—but as a figure constructed from his silences, his habits of listening, his relationship to the sounds coming through the radio speaker. We learn that he kept notebooks in which he copied passages from radio dramas. We learn that he had a radio schedule written on a piece of paper he kept in his shirt pocket. We learn that after his wife’s death, he listened to the same music program every evening for a decade. But we are not told what this meant to him, whether it was grief or habit or simple preference.

This refusal to psychologize is deliberate, and much of the book’s power derives from it. Mahmoud writes in English (she eventually emigrated to London), and there is something particularly acute about writing memoir about Cairo in a language that is not the language of her childhood. This distance, this slight displacement, seems to suit her subject. She can observe her father and her childhood Cairo with the intensity that intimacy often prevents.

The strongest sections come when Mahmoud describes the voices themselves. There is a long passage about the voice of a particular news announcer, a man whose diction and intonation became so familiar to her father that his absence was noticeable. When the man died, the broadcasts changed. No one announced this change; it simply occurred, and Omar noticed it immediately. Mahmoud attends to this moment without sentimentalizing it—she does not claim that this was a turning point or that it made her father sad. But something shifted, a small alteration in the texture of daily life.

There is also a fascinating chapter on radio advertising, particularly for products aimed at women. Mahmoud examines the language used to sell jam, fabric softener, and beauty products, and she connects this advertising voice to her mother’s own complicated relationship to modernity and consumption. The chapter is amusing and sharp, but it also carries an undertone of loss—the world of that advertising, those particular assumptions about women and domesticity, is now mostly gone.

Where the book occasionally falters is when Mahmoud attempts broader cultural-historical statements. She wants to connect the radio to Nasser-era nationalism, to Suez, to the Arab moment of the mid-century. But these connections are sometimes schematic, as if she is fulfilling an obligation rather than following genuine insight. The book is strongest when it stays close to the domestic, to the particular sounds and rhythms of her father’s life, and least convincing when it tries to be a social history of Egypt.

There is also a question about the reader’s relationship to this material. Mahmoud’s method—fragmented, associative, devoted to small details and overheard moments—is genuinely interesting but also somewhat ascetic. There are readers who will find this refreshing, a relief from the narrative machinery of conventional memoir. Others may find it frustratingly thin, a collection of observations rather than a lived experience fully rendered.

Yet there is something admirable about Mahmoud’s refusal to turn her father into a character, to make him cohere into a psychologically coherent figure. The radio, as she presents it, becomes a kind of record of attention itself: someone listening, someone present in a room, someone leaving traces in the form of notebooks and habits. It is a small monument to a life that did not demand public recognition, and in that insistence on smallness and quietness, there is a kind of dignity worth respecting.

Karim Nasr is a writer and critic based between Cairo and London. He is the author of “The Modern Arabic Essay: Form and Function” and has contributed to the Guardian, The National, and Boundary 2. His memoir of his own family’s migration is forthcoming.
