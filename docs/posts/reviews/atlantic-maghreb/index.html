The Turning Tide

A new history rescues Morocco from terrestrial thinking.

Reviews
History
Hassan Boumediene’s Atlantic Maghreb rewrites the Mediterranean as a space of Moroccan agency.
Author

Amina El-Mansouri

Published

February 28, 2026

Atlantic Maghreb: Morocco and the Sea, 1400–1900 by Hassan Boumediene · Oxford University Press · 548 pp · $85

The historiography of North Africa has long suffered from what we might call a landlocked imagination. Scholars have treated the Maghreb as a terrestrial region—a desert hinterland, an interior backwater—peripheral to the main currents of Mediterranean and Atlantic history. The sea, in this view, belonged to Europeans: to Portuguese conquistadors, Spanish galleys, French colonizers. Morocco was something to be crossed, not something that crossed. Hassan Boumediene’s formidable new study, Atlantic Maghreb, seeks to invert this perspective entirely. What emerges is a more complex and less teleological portrait of a society that was not merely the victim of oceanic history but an active participant in it, with its own maritime ambitions, technologies, and networks.

Boumediene, a historian at the Sorbonne, has worked through extraordinary archives: Portuguese naval records, Moroccan court documents, the logs of privateers, harbor records from Salé and Essaouira, letters of European merchants, and the accounts of diplomats and travelers. His central claim is that from the fifteenth century onward, Morocco was engaged in a sophisticated competition for Atlantic and Mediterranean dominion—a competition it both won and lost, depending on the era and the measure. Far from being a passive object of European expansion, Moroccan sultanates actively built fleets, sponsored privateering expeditions, and negotiated complex trade agreements from positions of considerable strength.

The book’s opening chapters establish this baseline. Boumediene shows how the Moroccan maritime expansion of the 1500s, often forgotten, was comparable in scope and ambition to the early stages of European maritime empires. The Saadians maintained a significant naval presence, patronized corsairs, and even conducted campaigns along the African coast. These were not desperate acts of a declining power but conscious strategies of territorial control and economic gain. A Moroccan ambassador in Rome complained, in effect, that Europeans treated Morocco as if it were not a proper maritime nation—a remark that reveals the consciousness of their own standing that Moroccan elites possessed.

Where Boumediene’s argument grows more provocative is in his treatment of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. He argues against the standard narrative of decline and passivity. Instead, he documents the rise of the Sallee Rovers, the sophisticated naval policies of the Alaouite dynasty, and the integration of Moroccan merchants into trans-Atlantic commerce. The privateering economy, in particular, receives sustained and sympathetic attention. Boumediene does not ask us to admire corsairs, but he does ask us to recognize them as rational economic agents operating in an era when the distinction between privateering and warfare was negligible, and when maritime raiding was a central feature of European commerce as well.

The book’s middle sections trace the establishment of European trading posts—Mogador, Tangier, Ceuta—and Boumediene’s analysis here is particularly valuable. He shows how these enclaves were not simply imposed on a helpless hinterland, but were often the result of negotiation, profitable to Moroccan merchants and rulers, and integrated into existing Moroccan commercial networks. This is not to deny European domination but to complicate it—to show that Moroccan elites were not passive victims but strategic actors, making rational choices about what degree of European presence suited their interests.

The nineteenth century brings what Boumediene calls “the reversal.” The gradual militarization of European navies, the rise of industrial capitalism, and the decline of Moroccan privateering combined to shift the balance decisively. But Boumediene’s account of this shift is notably unsentimental. He does not dramatize Morocco’s loss as a tragedy, nor does he suggest that resistance was ever truly possible. Instead, he traces the pragmatic choices of rulers confronting a changing world, the complex negotiations with European powers, and the internal Moroccan debates about how to respond to naval inferiority.

The book is substantially made, with useful maps, tables of ship inventories, and a comprehensive bibliography. The prose is clear, though occasionally Boumediene’s argument becomes tangled in complexity—there are moments where one wishes for more direct statement and less qualification. His heavy reliance on European source materials also means that we hear more from European observers of Moroccan maritime activity than from Moroccan voices themselves. This is partly unavoidable but worth noting.

More fundamentally, one might ask whether Boumediene has overcorrected for the terrestrial bias of previous historians. To insist on Moroccan agency and sophistication is necessary; but at times the book seems to attribute too much strategic intentionality to decisions that may have been reactive or circumstantial. The privateering economy, for instance, may have been less a deliberate state policy than an adaptation to economic necessity. Boumediene sometimes reads choices as strategies when they might have been improvisations.

Yet these quibbles are minor against the book’s considerable achievement. Atlantic Maghreb will be essential reading for historians of the Mediterranean and the Atlantic, and it should provoke broader reconsideration of how we narrate the global early modern period. Boumediene has returned Morocco to the map—not as a passive shore but as an actor in the making of oceanic modernity. That alone makes this an important book.

Amina El-Mansouri is a historian of the Mediterranean and the author of “Merchants and Ministers: Trade and Diplomacy in the Eighteenth-Century Maghreb.” She teaches at the University of Rabat and is completing a study of female merchants in Atlantic port cities.
