The Apricot Trees of Meknès

A short story.

Fiction
Short Story
An elderly woman inherits her late mother’s garden and discovers what it means to carry forward a legacy written in soil and seasons.
Author

Claire Messier

Published

January 15, 2026

The taxi driver had to ask twice for directions before Laila found the house. Forty years was a long time to be away from Meknès—long enough that the medina’s streets had shifted in her memory, though physically they remained unchanged, the same pale stone and shadowed archways, the same smell of olive oil and jasmine hanging above the narrow passages like an old prayer.

The house itself was smaller than she remembered. That was always the case, wasn’t it? The proportions of childhood never survived into adulthood intact. What she had remembered as grand—the courtyard with its olive-green zellige, the fountain’s chatter—appeared now almost modest, though no less beautiful for it. Beautiful in the way of small, true things.

Her mother had died in February. Laila had received word in Paris on a Tuesday morning, the sun falling through her kitchen window in a way that seemed profane given the news. She was seventy-three years old. She had three grown children, a position at the Sorbonne, a careful life that had been built incrementally, with French precision. Her mother had been eighty-nine, living alone in this house for more than fifteen years, ever since her father died. Their correspondence over that time had consisted of brief letters and, in recent years, telephone calls on Sunday evenings—pleasant, circumscribed conversations that avoided the larger subjects.

Now Laila stood in the courtyard and felt the sun on her face. The medina was quieter than it used to be, the touristic life having migrated to the new city. She was grateful for this quietness.

The house’s main feature was the garden—something that had been marginal in Laila’s childhood understanding of the place, and yet which appeared now to be its true purpose. Beyond the courtyard, through the small arched doorway, lay what must have been a third of an acre, walled on all sides by apricot trees. Her mother had filled the space between them with smaller plantings: fig, pomegranate, a single cherry tree that stood alone near the far wall like a work of deliberate art. There were herbs everywhere—rosemary, mint, thyme—and vegetables in raised beds arranged with mathematical precision. Everything was overgrown now. Everything had gone wild.

“Madame, you are staying long?” The woman who had been caring for her mother appeared in the doorway, her face lined with a practical sort of sympathy. “The garden,” she said, glancing beyond Laila with a knowing expression. “Madame Bennani, she loved it very much.”

Laila nodded. She realized suddenly that she barely knew anything about her mother’s life. Not the small details of it, anyway. Not the shape of her days.

“Every morning,” the woman continued, “she would walk here before breakfast. Every morning, even in the heat of August. She had notebooks. Many, many notebooks.”

That evening, Laila found them in the bedroom: thick exercise books with marble-patterned covers, the kind sold in schoolboy stalls. There were sixteen of them, stacked on a shelf above the bed. The handwriting was her mother’s—precise, elegant, the penmanship of someone from an era when handwriting was considered a form of integrity. The first notebook began in 1994, the year Laila’s father died. The last entry was dated three weeks before her mother’s death.

She opened randomly to a page in the middle. “January 3rd. The apricot that blooms earliest this year is near the south wall—the one I planted the summer we brought Laila home from the hospital. It is three days ahead of schedule. The temperature is unusual. I wonder if Laila notices such changes in Paris, or if she moves through the seasons without attending to them.”

Laila sat on the bed and wept. She wept not from sorrow exactly, but from the specific shame of living a life of abstraction while her mother had been here, measuring the slight progressions of seasons, writing them down in these careful, patient notebooks. Documenting something. Bearing witness.

She spent the next week in the garden. She pruned the overgrown trees, pulling the apricot branches back toward their proper forms. She cleared the weeds from the herb beds and reset the vegetables. She read through the notebooks, slowly, beginning with the first one. Her mother had been a scientist of small things. She recorded temperatures, rainfall, the dates of first and last frosts. She noted which varieties produced fruit earliest, which insects visited the flowers, how the quality of apricots changed year to year, season to season. There were sketches of trees with annotations in the margins. There were recipes for jam written in the same careful hand, calculations about yields, thoughts about the garden’s future.

In one entry from 1997, written in July: “I wonder what it would mean to give Laila something of this. But perhaps it is enough that it exists. Perhaps it is enough to plant something that others might harvest.”

By the end of the week, the garden had returned to something like order. Laila had booked a leave from the university. She had made an offer on a small flat in the new city. She had called her youngest son and told him, without entirely explaining why, that she would be spending several months in Morocco.

She bought a notebook identical to her mother’s and began to record her observations. She noted the exact moment each tree came into bloom, the angle of light at different hours, the names of birds she didn’t recognize and looked up in a guide. She wrote about the quality of the apricots as they ripened, the flavor changing subtly week by week as August progressed toward September. She wrote about the way silence sounded different here than in Paris, less like absence and more like presence—the silence of attention, of things growing.

In the evenings, she sat beneath the cherry tree and read her mother’s notebooks. She made notes of her own about what her mother had been trying to understand. She was a scholar—it was what she did, what she knew. But this was different. This was not the analysis of texts but the reading of a life, and she found herself moved by her mother’s precision in such a small and peripheral thing, this careful notation of what endures.

One evening in early September, Laila cut apricots from the south-wall tree—the one her mother had planted the summer of her birth—and made jam according to her mother’s recipe. The fruit was golden and perfect. As she stirred the pot, watching the liquid amber coalesce, she understood that her mother had been writing something more complex than observations. She had been composing a kind of love letter, addressed to the future, and to anyone who might come after and choose to read it.

The jam set perfectly. Laila labeled the jars with dates and the tree’s location. She arranged them on a shelf in the kitchen, where they caught the light. She added a new notebook to the collection on the shelf above the bed. She was, after all, her mother’s daughter.

Claire Messier is a lecturer in French literature at the University of Lyon. She has published scholarly articles on women’s epistolary traditions in nineteenth-century fiction, and is working on her first collection of short stories. She grew up in Aix-en-Provence and has spent the last two years renovating a house in the Rhône Valley.
