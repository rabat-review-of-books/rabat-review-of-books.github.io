The Sweetness Examined

Mona Khalil’s poems refuse easy consolation.

Reviews
Poetry
In Apricot Year, a Lebanese poet confronts time, memory, and the quotidian resilience of women.
Author

Mehdi Khribch

Published

January 20, 2026

Apricot Year by Mona Khalil · Dar Saqi · 96 pp · £14.99

Mona Khalil’s debut collection, Apricot Year, opens with an image of a woman sitting on a balcony in Beirut, watching a neighbor hang laundry. This is not the Lebanon of war photography or geopolitical analysis. It is the Lebanon of small gestures, domestic endurance, the minute adjustments of daily life in a place that has learned to accommodate ruin as a permanent feature of the landscape. The poem’s speaker observes without sentimentality—the colors of the clothespins, the mechanical repetition of the hanging—and in this observation discovers not meaning but a kind of temporary grace. It is an exemplary opening for a collection that treats the ordinary as a territory worth sustained attention.

Khalil, who studied literature at the American University of Beirut and has worked as a translator, writes in English, though her ear carries the cadences and presences of Arabic. The poems move between personal memory, political awareness, and linguistic play, without ever quite settling into any single mode. She seems allergic to the consolations of either personal narrative or political statement, and this resistance is, in itself, a form of integrity.

Many of the early poems concern families, particularly mothers and grandmothers. There is a poem about her grandmother’s hands, their competence and their weakness; another about a mother preparing food for a daughter leaving the country. Khalil approaches these scenes with anthropological precision, as if trying to understand what domestic routine might mean in a society where security is provisional and departure is always possible. In one poem, the simple act of preparing to travel becomes a meditation on what can be carried and what must be left behind—both materially and emotionally. The poem works through specific details: the folding of a dress, the arrangement of medications, the question of whether to bring photographs. What could have been maudlin instead becomes architectural, precise.

Yet there is also something verging on the abstract in Khalil’s method. She often withholds narrative clarity. A poem might begin in a kitchen and end in a library without explaining the transition. A memory might be interrupted by something that seems like philosophy. This can be disorientating—the reader sometimes wishes for more conventional continuity—but it also suggests Khalil’s conviction that consciousness itself is not linear, that the mind moves between registers without apology.

The title poem, which appears roughly in the center of the collection, is perhaps the strongest. It moves through the apricot season in Lebanon, using this fruit as an occasion to meditate on time, ripeness, and the proper moment. There is a wonderful stanza about the brief window in which apricots are ripe enough to eat—how quickly they pass from unripe to overripe, how little time is allowed for perfection. The poem resists the obvious move (a metaphor for youth, for fleeting happiness), but neither does it reject it entirely. Instead, it hovers in that ambiguity, treating the apricot with respect for its own nature while permitting it to carry symbolic weight. The opening line is nearly untranslatable in its delicacy.

Where the collection falters is in its second half. As the poems multiply, some lose the precision that characterizes the opening section. A few poems seem to strain for difficulty without earning it. In one sequence about language and translation, the poems become somewhat abstract, concerned with the gaps between words, and while the intellectual project is clear, the emotional engagement flags. The question of what to do with the abstractions of language, how to make them matter to a reader who is not already invested in such games, remains unresolved.

There are also moments where Khalil’s withheld narrative clarity becomes merely obscure. In several pieces, the reader wants more context—not dramatic context, necessarily, but some steadier ground from which the disjunctions might make sense. It is a delicate balance, and Khalil does not always find it.

Yet the collection is worth reading for its refusals as much as for its affirmations. Khalil refuses sentimentality. She refuses political statement. She refuses, for the most part, the therapeutic narrative in which poetry emerges from trauma and illuminates it. Instead, she offers something more austere and harder to name: the representation of consciousness moving through a difficult world, neither despairing nor hoping, but registering what it encounters with attention and linguistic care.

Her lines often turn on a single word or image: a color, a texture, the weight of something held. She does not often traffic in the grand gestures of contemporary lyric poetry. What she offers is smaller, quieter, and for readers patient enough to meet it on its own terms, more sustaining than grandeur often is.

Apricot Year announces a poet of real promise. What will be interesting to see is whether Khalil can sustain this level of attention across a longer work, and whether she can find ways to deepen the emotional reach without sacrificing the linguistic precision that makes these poems worth reading at all.

Michel Nasrallah is a poet, critic, and editor based in Beirut. He edits the literary journal Souk* and is the author of three collections of poetry in Arabic and English. His essays on contemporary Arab poetry have appeared in The Journal of Middle Eastern Literature and Boundary 2.*
