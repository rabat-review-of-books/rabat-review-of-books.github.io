The Bookseller of Rue du Consul

A short story.

Fiction
Short Story
An elderly Rabat bookseller receives a mysterious visitor seeking a book that may no longer exist, and discovers that the search itself is what matters.
Author

Mehdi Khribch

Published

March 8, 2026

The shop on Rue du Consul had not changed in forty years, which is to say that it had changed in all the ways that matter and none of the ways that are visible. The books were still arranged according to Karim’s personal taxonomy—a system that bore no resemblance to any known cataloguing method but which he navigated with the certainty of a man reading his own handwriting. Novels lived in the back left, organized by the year he had acquired them rather than by author or title. Poetry occupied a narrow shelf above the counter, each volume spine-marked with his own annotations. Travel narratives sprawled across an entire wall, alphabetized by destination rather than by name of author. Any visitor expecting logic would have left frustrated. Few visitors came anymore. Karim did not advertise. The shop existed as it always had—in the space between intention and accident, open at unpredictable hours, its window displaying books that had been there so long that the sunlight had bleached their spines to the color of old linen.

Karim himself was eighty-three years old. He had owned the shop since 1958, when he was young enough to believe that Rabat would become a great literary center, that men and women would gather in his shop to discuss Camus and Genet and the latest volumes from the Sorbonne. Instead, Rabat had become what most cities become—preoccupied with itself, no longer particularly interested in the thoughts of dead Europeans or the arguments of their heirs. The tourists came less frequently. Young people wanted their books on devices he did not understand. The other bookshops had closed, and he had remained, not from any particular stubbornness, but simply because he had not yet figured out how to stop.

The morning was warm. Karim sat behind his counter with a cup of strong coffee and a novel he had read at least seven times—a French edition of something by Patrick Modiano, a writer who seemed to understand that memory was a form of geography. He was on page forty-seven, which was far enough into the book to be comfortable, early enough that he could still be surprised by where the narrative might go. He had learned not to expect that surprise would refresh itself with rereading, but rather that rereading was its own form of surprise—the surprise of recognizing something that had always been there.

The woman entered in the late morning, when the light through the window had turned from pale to almost colorless. She was somewhere between sixty and seventy, dressed with the careful inattention of European women of her age—good clothes, but worn so well that one could not quite tell if they were chosen deliberately or had simply accumulated through years of use. She stood in the doorway for a moment, acclimating herself to the dimness, to the particular smell of the shop—which was not only books but something older, something like the smell of wood and time itself.

“Yes?” Karim asked, setting down his novel. “You are looking for something?”

“Perhaps,” she said. She had an accent that suggested France, but a France from some earlier moment, the vowels shaped differently than they were now. “I am looking for a book that may not exist. It may be out of print. It may never have been published. You are the bookseller?”

“I am the bookseller,” Karim confirmed. He gestured to the surrounding shelves. “Though you will have noticed that my organization is not conventional.”

“No,” she said, and something in her expression shifted, became almost amused. “No, it is not conventional. Which is fortunate, because the book I am looking for is not a conventional book.”

She moved further into the shop, and Karim watched her. She had the bearing of someone who had spent her life in libraries, or in museums, or in the kind of solitary work that required sustained attention. Her hands were careful as they moved along the spines.

“The book is a novel,” she continued. “French, I believe. Published—or possibly not published—in the early 1970s. The author was a woman, I think, though I am not entirely certain. The title had something to do with water. Or light. Or possibly both. I read it once, in circumstances that were not entirely conducive to careful attention, and I have been looking for it ever since. That was thirty years ago.”

Karim nodded slowly. In his experience, people looking for books usually knew what they were looking for with a kind of obsessive precision. This woman was describing something that might as well have been a dream. It made the search more interesting.

“Do you remember the plot?” he asked.

“There was a woman,” she said. “She lived in a city—I think it was Paris, but I am not certain. She was a translator. She was translating something from German, I believe. Or possibly Arabic. And there was a man. There was always a man. But that was not what the book was about.”

“What was it about?” Karim asked.

The woman paused. She was running her fingers along the spines of the novels in the back left, the ones organized by acquisition date. She seemed to be looking not at the books but through them, at some earlier vision.

“It was about forgetting,” she said finally. “It was about the way that we remember things imperfectly, and how that imperfection is not a failure of memory but rather a kind of truth. The character was trying to recover something that had been lost—a manuscript, I think. And the more she searched, the less certain she became that the manuscript had ever existed at all. By the end of the book, I could no longer tell if she was searching for the manuscript or if the manuscript was searching for her. Or if they were the same thing.”

Karim was quiet. He was thinking about a novel he had read in 1974, a slim volume in a gray cover with no author photograph. He had purchased it at an estate sale, from a woman who was clearing out her apartment before moving to Brussels. He had not thought of that book in years, but he remembered it now—the particular quality of its prose, the way the sentences seemed to fold back on themselves. He remembered being unable to decide, once he finished it, whether he had just read something extraordinary or something that was merely competent enough to seem extraordinary in one’s confused state after finishing a book.

“Wait,” he said. He rose, with the small difficulty that rising now entailed, and moved toward a shelf in the middle of the shop—the shelf dedicated to the 1970s, where books sat in the order of their arrival, each one a small moment of his past self’s decision-making.

He ran his fingers along the spines. The book was there, or perhaps it was there. The cover was gray. The lettering was small. The author’s name was something like Beaumont, or possibly Beaufort. He pulled it down and opened it to the title page.

“Anne Beaumont,” he said aloud. “Published in 1972 by a small press in Lyon. The title is ‘Les Lumières du Silence.’”

The woman’s expression changed. It was subtle, but Karim saw it—a kind of collapse, or perhaps a kind of settling into place, the way a building settles once its foundation is finally complete.

“Yes,” she whispered. “Yes, that is the title. That is the exact title.”

She took the book from him, her hands trembling slightly. She opened it, and Karim watched her read the first page, then the first paragraph, her face becoming younger as she did, becoming the face of the person who had first read this book thirty years ago, in circumstances that were not conducive to careful attention.

“I never found it again,” she said. “I have been looking. I have written to publishers. I have searched libraries. And I found nothing. I began to think that I had misremembered the title. That perhaps I had misremembered the entire book, invented it from fragments of things I had read.”

“But you found it,” Karim said quietly.

“Yes,” she said. “Through a man who keeps a bookshop on Rue du Consul in Rabat, organizing his inventory according to some private system that should not work and yet apparently does.” She looked up at him. “I do not believe in luck,” she said. “But I believe in something. I believe in the way that certain things find their way toward us, even when we are not looking for them.”

She purchased the book. Karim wrapped it carefully in brown paper, the way he wrapped all of his books, without tape, secured only by the precision of the fold. She paid him more than the book was worth, and he did not protest. They both understood that the transaction was not about currency.

At the door, she turned back. “Why did you keep it?” she asked. “The book? You might have sold it forty years ago. You might have forgotten it. Why did you keep it?”

Karim considered this. He thought about all the small acts of preservation that a human life contained, the books one kept not because one would reread them but because they had occupied a significant moment. He thought about memory, and about the imperfection of memory, and about how that imperfection was not a failure but a kind of truth.

“Because,” he said finally, “I thought that someday, someone might come looking for it.”

Sophie Lefort teaches literature at the Université de Toulouse and writes reviews of forgotten novels for several French literary journals. She has lived in Morocco for two years and is currently working on a collection of interconnected short stories set in the bookshops and libraries of North African cities. She is fascinated by the history of small presses and the lives of books that have fallen out of print.
