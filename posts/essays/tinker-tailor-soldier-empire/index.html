Tinker, Tailor, Soldier, Empire

On spymasters, gentlemen, and the violence they learned not to see.

Essays
Film
Empire
Cold War
The fantasy of the competent institution has always been empire in a better suit. A view from the receiving end.
Author

Mehdi Khribch

Published

April 12, 2026

In a wilderness of mirrors, what will the spider do? — T.S. Eliot, Gerontion

In Robert De Niro’s The Good Shepherd, that glacial film about the founding of the CIA, there occurs a moment of theological precision masked as conversation. Matt Damon’s Edward Wilson, equipped with the emotional carapace that only Yale and inherited wealth can provide, is posed a question about possession. What do the Protestant gentry claim to own? The Italians, the questioner suggests, have their families; the Jews their memory; the Irish their grievance. Wilson considers this without visible contemplation, then offers what appears to be a reply but is actually a statement of sovereignty: we have the United States of America; the rest of you are just visiting.

The remark operates not as paradox but as cadastral instrument. It is not offered boastfully; it is stated as one might state the earth’s rotation. And in this precision lies the whole architecture of American intelligence, and, from the vantage of those positioned to observe it, of the broader Anglo-American imperial system.

I have thought about that line for years. About its serene violence, its bureaucratic certainty, the way it sounds like an observation rather than a threat. I have thought about it the way one thinks of a closed building whose shadow perpetually falls across one’s street. Because for those of us who matured in the aftermath of what these gentlemen accomplished, in Rabat, Algiers, Kinshasa, Santiago, the question was never whether the CIA possessed competence. The question was whether competence, concentrated in imperial hands, might be indistinguishable from impunity.

The Poet-Spymaster

James Jesus Angleton occupied the CIA’s counterintelligence directorate from 1954 to 1975, commanding it as one might command a vast paranoid archive. Born in Boise, reared partly in Milan, educated at Yale, where he co-founded Furioso and published Pound, Eliot, Cummings. He moved in the circles of high modernists who believed attention itself a moral faculty. He imported their techniques into espionage: the close reading of Richards and Empson, their paranoid hermeneutics of ambiguity, the assumption that every surface masked a structure beneath. He was meant to be a translator of meaning; he became its saboteur. Brilliant, gaunt, chain-smoking, and ruinously wrong about almost everything consequential.

Thomas Powers, reviewing Morley’s biography in the London Review of Books, circled around the question biography could not quite answer: how did a reader as scrupulous as Angleton, trained in the exegesis of modernist verse, come to poison institutional intelligence with a counterintelligence operation that consumed a decade, destroyed careers, and discovered no moles? Powers suggests the disaster lay not despite but because of Angleton’s literary sensibility. He read the world as he had learned to read Four Quartets: assuming surfaces concealed depths, that every testimony was potentially a dangle, that silence itself was a legible text. The KGB’s “Monster Plot,” which obsessed him, was ultimately his own text, author and sole reader alike, bound within a hermeneutic circle no evidence could penetrate. He had confused intelligence work with textual exegesis. He had applied the aesthetics of modernism to a domain where aesthetic methods produce paranoia rather than understanding.

He was meant to be a translator of meaning; he became its saboteur.

Fiction has apprehended this more clearly than the historiography. The novel has always known what to do with men like Angleton; the archival impulse tends to domesticate them. Edward Wilson in The Good Shepherd is a composite, but Angleton provides its skeleton: the orchid cultivator, the Yale man who believed that civilisation’s defence necessitated the systematic destruction of civilised constraint. De Niro’s film grasps, with its characteristic slowness, what institutional history obscures: that the Skull and Bones ritual and the waterboarding of a prisoner in some Central American cell are not contradictions but iterations. The same violent gesture performed at different altitudes of social permission, with different registers of concealment.

The Conspiracy as Literary Form

Angleton’s genuine afterlife resides in American literature. Don DeLillo’s Libra remains the most rigorous exploration of how intelligence operations warp the fabric of causality itself. The novel does not resolve the Kennedy assassination; it narrates instead the convergence of intentions, showing how individual plots and institutional logics can produce events that no single actor fully authors. The CIA functionaries in Libra are not visionaries but middle managers of entropy; they initiate processes, then watch them acquire momentum beyond prediction or control. Oswald becomes neither their instrument nor their puppet but their refraction, a man equally persuaded that individual will can bend history.

DeLillo finds his dialectical partner in James Ellroy, whose American Tabloid and The Cold Six Thousand collapse the FBI, the CIA, the Mafia, and the Kennedy administration into a single power organism, a hydra whose heads wage war while sharing blood. Ellroy’s staccato syntax, those telegraphic forensic sentences, erases the institutional veneer and exposes the transactional brutality beneath. The Kennedy assassination in his telling is not a puzzle to be solved but a convergence to be narrated: organised crime, Cuban exiles, agency rogue actors, and the delusional patriotism of a state waging coups across continents while claiming to defend freedom at home.

The Warren Commission was not an investigative failure. It was a containment success.

Both writers recognise what cinema also apprehends: that conspiracy theory is not democracy’s pathology but its unconscious register. Americans orbit Dallas 1963 as the analysand returns to the primal scene; not seeking truth but narrating the unbearable knowledge that institutions are not what they profess. The Warren Commission was not an investigative failure; it was a containment success. Angleton, who possessed more intelligence about the assassination than he ever disclosed, who monitored Oswald’s dossier, who orchestrated the CIA’s Warren Commission liaison, understood containment with the precision of a man trained in textual suppression. He was, fundamentally, a close reader. He knew that a text reveals itself not through what it states but through what it must hide in order to maintain coherence.

The Berlin Wall: the Cold War's most literal partition, and the set on which le Carré staged his moral theatre.

Le Carré’s Circus and Its Discontents

If Angleton is American intelligence’s literary apotheosis, George Smiley is its British antithesis. Le Carré, who served in both MI5 and MI6, stationed in the divided Cold War cities, witness to Philby’s devastation of the service, created in Smiley a figure of moral gravity and institutional exhaustion. Smiley is Angleton inverted: self-scrutinising where Angleton was certain; unglamorous where Angleton cultivated mystique; morally wounded by what he comprehends. Yet he serves the Circus, le Carré’s fictionalised MI6, with the loyalty of a man for whom departure has become impossible.

Alfredson renders this in Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy (2011) with architectural precision. Gary Oldman’s Smiley operates in near silence, occupying rooms drained of colour: beige, nicotine-stain, the grey of institutional corridors and filing systems. He listens. The film’s chromatic world is one that has discarded faith in its own legitimacy but continues, through habit and residual obligation, enacting its ceremonies. When the mole is exposed, there comes no sense of resolution. Only the apprehension that treachery is the system’s foundational condition, that compromise preceded the institution, that those who administered it recognised this and elected blindness because the alternative, acknowledging the edifice’s construction on void, was ontologically impossible.

Le Carré’s true obsession was never espionage but England; more precisely, the ethical dissolution of the English ruling apparatus, its public school convictions decomposing from within, its assumption that civilisation was a British commodity the world owed gratitude for receiving. Germany becomes Smiley’s “second soul,” the nation that had demonstrated catastrophically what occurs when civilisational presumption exhausts its restraints. Germany was the mirror England would not examine.

Philby's treason was not systemic malfunction but systemic consequence: the inevitable outcome of a closed order that could not question its own.

Kim Philby emerges from this reflection as its most consequential laceration. Son of St John Philby the Arabist, recruited by Moscow in 1934 and elevated to MI6’s highest echelons, he betrayed operations, networks, agents across three decades. His treason was not systemic malfunction but systemic consequence: the inevitable outcome of a closed world organised around class privilege and the assumption that one’s own kind could not be enemy. To credit Philby’s betrayal was to question the order’s legitimacy itself. Le Carré, who knew Philby, whose career bore Philby’s scar, would spend his literary life anatomising one recurrent figure: the gentleman servant of an institution he knows has already betrayed him, and who serves it still.

Kim Philby at his 1955 press conference, denying he was the 'third man.' He would defect to Moscow eight years later.

The View from the Receiving End

This is where I stand: Rabat, the Maghreb, what Yale and Cambridge would designate with measured condescension as the Third World. From this vantage, the mythology crystallises differently. Angleton tending orchids, Smiley polishing spectacles, Wilson sacrificing intimacy to institutional duty: these figures dissolve into what they are. A costume drama constructed from a geography of bones.

Morocco’s intelligence history is not subsidiary to this narrative. It is the central text, in a language the spymasters chose not to learn. The Years of Lead, les années de plomb, stretched from the 1960s through the late 1980s: thousands of dissidents imprisoned, vanished, tortured, killed. This machinery required coordination. Hassan II’s Morocco was not incidental to Western Cold War strategy; it was integral. A valued ally on the North African flank. The Safari Club, that informal constellation of intelligence services from France, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, Morocco, and the CIA, operated across Africa in the 1970s conducting covert operations beyond formal authorisation or oversight, toppling governments and suppressing movements that threatened the post-colonial arrangement of power. Mehdi Ben Barka’s kidnapping and assassination in Paris (1965), by agents of his own security apparatus in coordination with French intelligence and likely CIA knowledge, signalled how these lines of allegiance operated. Cold War logic penetrated every institution; the Eastern bloc was the stated enemy, but nationalist movements and leftist organising were treated as equally existential threats.

The CIA helped reorganise Morocco’s security apparatus in the early 1960s. What followed was design, not accident. Langley’s counterintelligence operations intersected with Rabat’s repression not as separate events but as expressions of a unified strategic orientation.

John le Carré with Le Miroir aux Espions, the French edition of The Looking Glass War. His subject was never espionage; it was England.

What Angleton performed in his office in Langley, reading each defector’s testimony as modernist exegesis, constructing vaulted architectures of suspicion, acquired its real political anatomy in Casablanca and Rabat, in Derb Moulay Cherif and Tazmamart. While he hunted phantoms in Washington, real people faced interrogation in Moroccan cells. Leftist movements, trade unions, intellectuals, insurgent poets: none were collateral casualties. All were designated targets, processed through security services equipped, trained, and authorised by the very intelligence establishments whose institutional neuroses le Carré anatomised.

And it was not only Morocco. In the Congo, Patrice Lumumba was assassinated with CIA complicity. In Ghana, Kwame Nkrumah was overthrown. In Chile, in Indonesia, in Iran: the pattern repeated itself with the regularity of a bureaucratic procedure, which is exactly what it was. The coups, the assassinations, the propped-up dictatorships were not failures of the system. They were the system. The gentleman spy, the Yale man, the Oxbridge recruit was not defending civilisation against barbarism. He was defending a particular arrangement of global power against those who questioned it, and he did so with the serene confidence of a man taught, at the best schools in the world, that his judgement was synonymous with the good.

The Surrealist Moment

The mythology of the competent institution, that fantasy of wood-panelled rooms where serious men in good suits manage global affairs with quiet precision, has suffered a blow from which recovery seems unlikely. The blow was not delivered by the Global South, not by empire’s deferred accounting, but by the empire itself.

Trump is neither Angleton nor Smiley nor Wilson. He is the moment when concealment ceases to function as operational necessity. The structure beneath the veneer reveals itself hollow: not a conspiracy of competent men but naked machinery of self-interest, requiring no civilisational alibi. Where Angleton deployed literary criticism against Soviet intelligence, Trump applies real estate transaction logic. Where le Carré’s men agonised in silence, Trump broadcasts. The deception is not buried; the corruption sits on surfaces. We have arrived at DeLillo’s dark vision made literal: a government requiring no concealments because concealment has lost operational value.

The espionage novel cannot process this. The genre depends on secrecy, on buried truth requiring excavation. But what occurs when truth is shouted from podiums? When the president conducts foreign policy as a protection racket and half the country consents? The Circus maintained the fiction of serving something beyond itself; the current arrangement has abandoned even that gesture. Power pursuing power; corruption without hypocrisy’s grace. For those already sceptical of the old order, it produces an unsettling affect: something like nostalgia for spymasters who at least possessed the stylistic decency to lie with elegance.

The facade and what it conceals: institutional architecture as a theory of legitimacy.

But not quite. The old order was never innocent. Its elegance was its pathology. Its competence was its crime. Angleton’s close reading was more dangerous than Trump’s illiteracy precisely because it arrived wrapped in intelligence and cultural prestige, producing brutality in the torture chambers of allied regimes, brutality no less real for being administered by men who had read Pound. The gentlemen of the Circus, for all their moral suffering, managed an imperial system that extracted wealth, suppressed movements, and installed compliant regimes across the Global South with an efficiency Trump has not yet approached. The difference is aesthetic rather than moral. And aesthetics, as Angleton should have understood better than anyone, are profoundly unreliable guides to truth.

A Wilderness Without Mirrors

From Rabat, the lesson is this: intelligence agencies are not fundamentally cynical. Cynicism requires self-knowledge; belief does not. The pathology was not cynicism but conviction: what was being done was good, and this conviction was itself the operation, the one conducted by empire upon its own officers to blind them to what it required them to do.

Le Carré traced this trajectory in his later novels: pharmaceutical extraction in Africa, arms trafficking in the Gulf, mechanisms of global capital replacing Cold War aesthetics. The Circus was not British but the template: a metaphor for ordering the world as administration rather than violence, as technique rather than coercion.

The orchids are dead. The files are declassified. The novels are written. Yet in the medinas of the Maghreb, the barrios of Latin America, the townships of southern Africa, their shadows persist. The wilderness they constructed remains inhabited. The espionage genre gave us the consolation of narrative: the mole is found, the file is closed, the weary spy goes home. History extends no such mercy. The file remains open. The institutional structure was itself the mole.

Mehdi Khribch is the editor of the Rabat Review of Books. This essay draws on his ongoing work on James Jesus Angleton and the literary imagination of intelligence.
